How Streaming Platforms Predict What You'll Love Next
Collaborative Filtering — Learning from the Crowd
The core insight: users who agreed in the past tend to agree in the future. 
Rather than analyzing what a song sounds like, CF looks at what people do — streams, skips, playlist adds, likes — to find taste neighbors.

How it works:

Matrix factorization: Builds a giant user-song matrix and decomposes it into latent "taste" vectors for both users and songs. 
                        A user vector dotted with a song vector produces a predicted affinity score.
k-Nearest Neighbors: Find users most similar to you, then surface songs they loved that you haven't heard.
Spotify's twist: Treats 700M user-generated playlists as "sentences" and songs as "words" — 
                    running a Word2Vec-style model so that songs appearing on the same playlists end up close together in embedding space. 
                    This powers Discover Weekly.
Strength: Discovers music outside your comfort zone by surfacing what taste-twins love.
Weakness: Fails completely for new users or new songs with no history (the cold-start problem).

Content-Based Filtering — Analyzing the Music Itself
The core insight: recommend songs that sound like songs you already like. No other users required.

How it works:

Audio feature extraction: Spotify extracts features like danceability, energy, valence, tempo, acousticness, speechiness, and up to 
                            42 internal dimensions per track.
CNNs on raw audio: For brand-new songs with no play history, Spotify runs convolutional neural networks directly on audio spectrograms to generate a "pseudo-listen" embedding — 
                    placing the song in recommendation space before any human has heard it.
NLP on cultural context: Spotify also ingests playlist titles, blog posts, lyrics, and social media to extract cultural tags 
                        (e.g., "melancholic acoustic") using LLMs.
Taste profile: Your profile is the aggregate feature vector of songs you've liked. New songs nearest to that centroid get surfaced.
Strength: Works immediately for new songs and new users who state explicit preferences.
Weakness: Tends toward a filter bubble — locking you into the attributes of music you already know.

How Platforms Combine Both (Hybrid Systems)
Platform	Approach
Spotify	3-layer hybrid: collaborative embeddings + CNN audio embeddings + NLP cultural vectors, all fused into a universal foundation layer. 
                        Uses contextual bandits (BaRT framework) on the homepage. LLM-generated explanation narratives boosted clicks on niche content 4x.
YouTube Music	Two-tower neural model: one tower embeds users (watch history, queries), one embeds tracks (content + metadata). kNN retrieves candidates, then a transformer ranker re-ranks by modeling which parts of your history are contextually relevant.
Apple Music	"Algo-torial" — ML recommendations reviewed by human music editors for quality control and cultural nuance.
Deezer	Treats playlist building as a sequence-to-sequence problem with transformer models; researching LLM-based recommendation.
Key Tradeoffs
Collaborative Filtering	Content-Based Filtering	Hybrid/Neural
Cold start (new user)	Fails	Works from stated prefs	Onboarding questionnaires
Cold start (new song)	Fails	Works immediately	CNN audio bridges the gap
Filter bubble risk	High	High	Mitigated with diversity constraints
Serendipity	Moderate (via taste neighbors)	Low	High (bandit exploration)
Interpretability	Low	Moderate	Very low
Filter bubble reality check: A 2024 study of ~50K Deezer users found algorithms can produce both confinement and exploration depending on context — it's not as simple as "algorithms always narrow taste." Platforms inject serendipity via exploration bonuses in bandit algorithms, diversity caps in ranking, and LLM-mediated explanations that make unexpected recommendations feel less jarring.

This directly maps to the patterns in your recommender.py — the song attribute matching is a classic content-based approach, and adding user-similarity logic would move it toward collaborative filtering.
